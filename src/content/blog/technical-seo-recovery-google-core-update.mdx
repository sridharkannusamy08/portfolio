# Technical SEO Recovery: How I Restored 85% Organic Traffic After a Google Core Update

When a Google core update hits, most businesses panic. Their agency says "wait it out." Months pass. Traffic doesn't recover.

That's exactly what happened to my UK client — until they called me.

This is the full story of how I diagnosed and fixed the issues that caused a 60% traffic drop, and recovered 85% of it within 8 weeks.

---

## The Situation

A UK-based wellness brand contacted me in a state of crisis. Three weeks after Google's core algorithm update, their organic traffic had dropped by 60%. Revenue from the organic channel collapsed.

Their previous SEO agency's response? "Core updates are unpredictable. Give it 3–6 months."

That's not an answer. That's an excuse.

---

## The Diagnosis: Full Technical SEO Audit

My first action was a complete technical audit. I used a combination of Screaming Frog, Google Search Console, PageSpeed Insights, and manual analysis.

Here's what I found:

### Issue 1: Crawl Inefficiency (340+ Errors)
The site had:
- 127 broken internal links (404 errors)
- 89 redirect chains (A → B → C instead of A → C)
- 43 pages with duplicate title tags
- Crawl budget being wasted on paginated URLs without canonicals

**Priority: Critical**

### Issue 2: Duplicate Content Crisis
Multiple URL patterns were creating duplicate pages:
- `example.com/product` and `example.com/product?sort=price` indexed as separate pages
- Category pages with and without trailing slashes
- HTTP and HTTPS versions of the same pages (yes, in 2024)

**Priority: Critical**

### Issue 3: Core Web Vitals Failure
- LCP (Largest Contentful Paint): 4.8 seconds (Poor)
- CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift): 0.34 (Poor)
- FID: 280ms (Needs Improvement)

The LCP was caused by an unoptimised hero image loading with no lazy loading and no WebP format.

**Priority: High**

### Issue 4: E-E-A-T Signals Missing
After Google's helpful content updates, E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) signals became more critical:
- Author bios were missing from all blog posts
- No credentials or qualifications mentioned
- Product pages had no reviews schema
- No "About the Brand" content demonstrating expertise

**Priority: High**

### Issue 5: Thin Content
43 pages had fewer than 300 words. Google's helpful content system penalises low-value content that doesn't serve user intent.

**Priority: Medium**

---

## The Recovery Plan: 8-Week Roadmap

### Weeks 1–2: Critical Technical Fixes

**Crawl Errors:**
Fixed all 127 broken links. Updated redirect chains to single-hop redirects. Implemented proper pagination canonicals.

**Duplicate Content:**
Set canonical tags across all product and category pages. Resolved the HTTP/HTTPS duplication with 301 redirects. Standardised trailing slash policy and 301-redirected variants.

### Weeks 3–4: Core Web Vitals

**LCP Fix:**
- Converted hero image to WebP format (57% file size reduction)
- Added `priority` attribute to above-the-fold images
- Implemented server-side rendering for LCP elements

Result: LCP dropped from 4.8s → 1.9s

**CLS Fix:**
- Added explicit `width` and `height` attributes to all images
- Fixed dynamic content that caused layout shifts

**FID/INP Fix:**
- Deferred non-critical JavaScript
- Removed 3 unused analytics scripts

### Weeks 5–6: Content & E-E-A-T

**Author Profiles:**
Added detailed author bios to all blog posts including credentials, expertise, and author image.

**Schema Markup:**
Implemented:
- `Product` schema with reviews and ratings
- `Organization` schema
- `BreadcrumbList` schema
- `Article` schema on blog posts

**Content Enhancement:**
Identified the 43 thin pages, expanded 20 priority pages to 800+ words with genuine useful content.

### Weeks 7–8: Internal Linking Architecture

Rebuilt the internal linking structure based on topical authority clusters. Every service/product page now has 5+ contextually relevant internal links from high-authority pages.

This significantly improved PageRank distribution across the site.

## Key Lessons for Your Website

**1. Core updates are not random.** They target specific quality signals. Audit your content, E-E-A-T, and technical health immediately after an update.

**2. Crawl budget matters more than you think.** If Google's bot wastes time on broken links and redirect chains, important pages get crawled less frequently.

**3. Core Web Vitals are a ranking factor.** Not the most important one — but when your competitors are all similar in content quality, page experience becomes the tiebreaker.

**4. E-E-A-T is not optional in 2025.** Who wrote this content? Why should I trust it? If your site can't answer those questions, you're vulnerable.
